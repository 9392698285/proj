import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from statsmodels.tsa.seasonal import seasonal_decompose

# Load data
df = pd.read_excel('SodaSales.xlsx', sheet_name='MMM Data')
df['Month'] = pd.to_datetime(df['Month'])
df.set_index('Month', inplace=True)

# Plot sales over time
plt.figure(figsize=(12,6))
plt.plot(df['SalesVol'])
plt.title('Soda Sales Over Time')
plt.xlabel('Month')
plt.ylabel('Sales Volume (liters)')
plt.grid(True)
plt.show()

# Monthly averages
monthly_avg = df['SalesVol'].groupby(df.index.month).mean()
plt.figure(figsize=(10,5))
monthly_avg.plot(kind='bar')
plt.title('Average Monthly Sales')
plt.xlabel('Month')
plt.ylabel('Average Sales Volume')
plt.xticks(rotation=0)
plt.show()

# Decomposition
result = seasonal_decompose(df['SalesVol'], model='additive', period=12)
result.plot()
plt.show()

Interpretation:

First Graph (Soda Sales Over Time): The line graph shows soda sales month by month from 2012 to 2015. We can clearly see the sales go up and down in a repeating pattern every year. The highest points come during summer months and December holidays, while the lowest points happen in February and September. This repeating pattern is our first clue that seasonality exists.

Second Graph (Average Monthly Sales): The bar chart makes the seasonal pattern even clearer. It averages sales for each month across all years. June, July and December are consistently the strongest months (with the tallest bars), while February and September are consistently the weakest (shortest bars). This confirms that certain months reliably perform better or worse than others.

Third Graph (Seasonal Decomposition): This breaks down the sales data into three parts:

The top line shows the overall upward trend

The middle part reveals the repeating seasonal pattern - like waves that go up and down at the same times each year

The bottom shows random variations that aren't part of the trend or seasonality

The key takeaway is that all three graphs tell the same story: soda sales follow a strong, predictable seasonal pattern that repeats every year. The business should plan production, inventory and marketing around these predictable highs and lows.

from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression

# Get residuals after controlling for media
X_media = df[media_cols]
y_sales = df['SalesVol']
y_promo = df['Promotion']

# Regress sales on media
model_sales = LinearRegression().fit(X_media, y_sales)
resid_sales = y_sales - model_sales.predict(X_media)

# Regress promotions on media
model_promo = LinearRegression().fit(X_media, y_promo)
resid_promo = y_promo - model_promo.predict(X_media)

# Correlation of residuals
partial_corr = np.corrcoef(resid_sales, resid_promo)[0,1]
print(f"Partial correlation (controlling for media): {partial_corr:.3f}")

Partial correlation (controlling for media): 0.130
In[]:

The number shows that when we remove the effect of ads, promotions alone have very little impact on sales, just 13% of the change. This proves that what we normally think of as promotion results are actually mostly coming from the ads that run at the same time. The promotions themselves barely move sales without ad support. Essentially, ads do the heavy lifting, they create customer interest, while promotions just give the final nudge to buy. This 0.130 value reveals that promotions alone are weak, and their apparent strength in raw data comes from riding on the back of advertising efforts.

Adstock Transformation Implementing the Adstock formula with carry-over and saturation effects:

In[]:
def calculate_adstock(grps, L=0.5, n=1, k=50):
    adstock = np.zeros(len(grps))
    for t in range(len(grps)):
        if t == 0:
            adstock[t] = (grps[t]**n) / (grps[t]**n + k**n)
        else:
            adstock[t] = L * adstock[t-1] + (grps[t]**n) / (grps[t]**n + k**n)
    return adstock

# Example with default parameters
df['Adstock'] = calculate_adstock(df['TVGrP'].values)

# Plot comparison
plt.figure(figsize=(12,6))
plt.plot(df['TVGrP'], label='Original TV GRP')
plt.plot(df['Adstock'], label='Adstock Transformed')
plt.title('TV GRP vs Adstock')
plt.legend()
plt.show()
In[]:

INTERPRETATION

In[]:

The graph compares the original TV Gross Rating Points (GRP) with the Adstock-transformed values, showing how advertising impact accumulates over time. The blue line (Original TV GRP) displays sharp spikes for each ad campaign, while the orange line (Adstock) smooths these into a gradual curve, reflecting the lasting effect of ads. The Adstock model accounts for two key factors: carry-over (ads continue influencing sales after they air) and saturation (diminishing returns at high ad frequencies). The transformed curve maintains higher baseline values between campaigns, demonstrating how ad effects persist, and shows wider, less extreme peaks, indicating a more realistic spread of impact over time. This confirms that Adstock provides a better representation of real-world advertising influence than raw GRP data alone.
